Section 1

1. Why Attention was needed

In Sequence Learning we saw that RNNs, LSTMs and GRUs read text one step at a time. This worked better than normal feed-forward networks because the model had a memory-like hidden state. But language often needs long-distance connections. A word near the end of a sentence may depend on a word near the beginning.

Example: Deepak, after preparing slides, checking the demo, fixing the laptop, and arranging the projector, entered the room because he had to teach AI. To understand he, the model must connect it back to Deepak, even though many words come in between.

Attention was created to reduce this difficulty. Instead of forcing all information to travel through one hidden state, Attention allows a token to directly look at other useful tokens. The word he can directly focus on Deepak.

Attention idea:
For this token, which other tokens should I focus on?
Section 2

2. Problem with remembering long sequences

Long sequences are hard because every new word may add new information. If a model must keep everything in one running memory, older information can become weak. Important context can be diluted by many later words.

This is especially painful in translation, summarization, reasoning over documents, and pronoun reference. A token may need to connect with something that appeared many tokens earlier. Attention gives a more direct path for that connection.

Without Attention:
word 1 → hidden state → hidden state → hidden state → word 30

With Attention:
word 30 can directly look back at word 1
Section 3

3. RNN hidden state bottleneck

In an RNN-style model, the hidden state is like a memory box. Every time a new token arrives, the box is updated. The problem is that a long sentence may contain many details, but the hidden state has limited capacity.

The model must compress subject, object, time, location, grammar, and meaning into a single vector. This compression creates a bottleneck. Some information may survive, but some information can become blurred or forgotten.

Attention reduces this bottleneck by keeping token representations available and letting each token collect information from the tokens it needs.

Section 4

4. LSTM helped, but did not fully solve it

LSTM and GRU improved RNNs using gates. They helped the model keep useful information longer and forget less useful information. This was a major improvement over basic RNNs.

But even with LSTM, information still flows step by step. If a sentence is very long, the model still has to carry information through many transitions. Attention gave a different solution: do not depend only on memory carried through steps; allow direct token-to-token focus.

LSTM improves memory.
Attention changes the access pattern.
Instead of only carrying memory, tokens can directly retrieve useful context.
Section 5

5. Translation problem example

Attention became famous in machine translation. Suppose the English sentence is I love India and the model is generating Hindi. When it generates the word corresponding to India, it should focus strongly on India in the source sentence. When it generates the word corresponding to love, it should focus strongly on love.

This alignment problem is hard if the whole source sentence is compressed into one vector. Attention lets the decoder look back at relevant source words while generating each output word.

Generated ideaShould focus on
भारतIndia
प्यारlove
मैं / मुझेI
Section 6

6. Human attention intuition

When humans read, we do not give equal importance to every word. If someone says, “Open the file we created yesterday and update the README,” your mind focuses on file, created yesterday, update, and README.

Words like “the,” “we,” and “and” are still part of the sentence, but they may not be the main focus for the current task. Attention gives models a similar selective focus mechanism, but mathematically using vectors and weights.

Section 7

7. Attention as selective focus

Attention means selective focus. It does not mean the model ignores all other words completely. It means the model gives different importance to different tokens.

For a current token, the model calculates how relevant every other token is. Some tokens get high weight, some get medium weight, and some get very low weight. Then the model mixes information according to these weights.

Attention = not equal focus
Attention = weighted focus
Section 8

8. Attention is not equal focus

If every word received equal attention, the model would not be able to resolve context well. In the sentence The dog chased the ball because it was rolling, the word it should focus more on ball than on dog.

But in The dog chased the ball because it was tired, the word it should focus more on dog. Same word, different attention pattern. This is why equal focus is not enough.

Section 9

9. Every word can look at other words

In self-attention, every token gets a chance to look at every other token in the same sequence. This creates a relationship map between tokens.

For example, in The student solved the problem because it was easy, the word it may attend to problem, while solved may attend to student and problem. Each token builds its own context-aware meaning.

Current tokenMay focus on
itproblem, easy
solvedstudent, problem
studentsolved
easyproblem
Section 10

10. Same word, different context

One of the strongest benefits of Attention is contextual meaning. The word bank can mean a financial institution or the side of a river. The starting embedding may contain broad possibilities, but attention helps refine the meaning using surrounding words.

SentenceUseful contextLikely meaning
He deposited money in the bank.deposited, moneyfinancial bank
The boat reached the river bank.boat, riverriver side

Attention helps the token update its representation based on the context around it.

Section 11

11. Pronoun reference problem

Pronouns are a simple way to understand Attention. Words like he, she, it, and they often depend on earlier nouns.

Example: The bottle fell because it was heavy. Here it refers to bottle. But in The table broke because it was weak, it refers to table. Attention helps the model connect the pronoun to the likely source token.

Invisible attention line:
it → bottle
it → table
Section 12

12. Meaning depends on surrounding words

A token by itself is often incomplete. The word light may refer to brightness or weight. The word mouse may refer to an animal or a computer device. The surrounding words decide the practical meaning.

Attention creates a new representation that contains the token plus useful context. So the model does not only represent mouse; it represents mouse in this sentence.

Section 13

13. Attention scores

The first step in Attention is calculating raw scores. A score answers: how strongly does the current token match another token?

Suppose the sentence is Deepak teaches AI and the current token is teaches. The model compares teaches with Deepak, teaches, and AI.

Current tokenLooking atRaw score
teachesDeepak4
teachesteaches5
teachesAI6

These scores are not percentages yet. They are raw compatibility values. Higher score means stronger potential relevance.

Section 14

14. Attention weights

Raw scores must be converted into attention weights. Weights are easier to interpret because they are positive and add up to 1.

For example, raw scores [4, 5, 6] may become weights [0.09, 0.24, 0.67]. This means the current token gives 9% focus to the first token, 24% to the second token, and 67% to the third token.

Attention weights are the model’s focus distribution for one current token.

Section 15

15. Softmax in attention

Softmax is the function usually used to convert raw attention scores into attention weights. It turns a list of numbers into probability-like values.

Useful properties of softmax: all outputs are positive, all outputs add up to 1, and bigger input scores get much bigger share than smaller scores.

softmax(score_i) = e^(score_i) / sum(e^(all scores))

[4, 5, 6] → approximately [0.09, 0.24, 0.67]

This is why Attention can clearly focus more on important tokens while still keeping small influence from others.

Section 16

16. Weighted sum intuition

After weights are calculated, Attention mixes information from tokens. This is done using a weighted sum of Value vectors.

If the current token is teaches, and weights are 0.09 for Deepak, 0.24 for teaches, and 0.67 for AI, then the output is a mixture of those token values.

Attention output =
0.09 × Value(Deepak)
+ 0.24 × Value(teaches)
+ 0.67 × Value(AI)

This is the core of Attention. It does not only choose a word; it creates a new context-aware vector.

Section 17

17. Context vector

The weighted sum produces a context vector. This vector contains information gathered from relevant tokens. It is not a normal English sentence; it is a numerical representation that the model can pass to later layers.

For example, after attention, the representation of it may carry information from ball and rolling. This helps later layers understand that it most likely refers to ball.

Section 18

18. Attention output

The output of Attention is a new vector for each token. Before Attention, a token has its starting embedding. After Attention, it has a context-aware representation.

Before AttentionAfter Attention
bank = broad token meaningbank + loan + approved context
it = pronoun meaningit + ball/road/dog context
teaches = action meaningteaches + subject/object context

This is why Attention is more than highlighting. It changes the internal representation of tokens.

Section 19

19. Word embedding before attention

An embedding gives a token its starting numeric meaning. It is like the model’s first guess about what the token may mean.

But starting embeddings are not enough because language is contextual. The same word can have different meanings in different sentences. Embeddings provide the base; Attention adjusts the base using context.

Section 20

20. Word embedding after attention

After Attention, a token vector includes information from surrounding useful tokens. This is called contextual representation.

For example, bank near loan becomes more financial. bank near river becomes more geographical. The token vector is updated based on what it attended to.

Embedding = base meaning
Attention = context adjustment
Section 21

21. Query, Key, Value intuition

Query, Key, and Value are the three famous terms in Attention. They sound technical, but the analogy is simple.

Imagine a library. You ask, “I want a book about AI.” Your question is the Query. Each book has a title or label; that is the Key. The actual content inside the book is the Value.

TermSimple meaning
QueryWhat am I looking for?
KeyWhat information do I contain?
ValueWhat information will I contribute?
Section 22

22. Query means what am I looking for

Every token creates a Query vector. The Query represents what the current token is searching for in the surrounding context.

For example, for the token it in The bottle fell because it was heavy, the Query may be looking for an object that can be referred to by a pronoun and can be heavy. The token bottle has a Key that matches this well.

Query(it): What object am I referring to?
Section 23

23. Key means what information do I contain

Every token creates a Key vector. The Key acts like a searchable label. It tells other tokens what kind of information this token may provide.

For example, the token bottle may have a Key indicating that it is an object and can be described by properties like heavy, broken, empty, or full. This makes it match the Query from it.

Section 24

24. Value means what information will I contribute

The Value vector is the actual information that will be passed forward if the token receives attention. Query and Key decide the match. Value provides the content.

If it attends strongly to bottle, then the Value of bottle is mixed into the representation of it. After that, the model can treat it more like bottle in this context.

Query-Key decides weight.
Value contributes information.
Section 25

25. Query-Key matching

Attention score is usually calculated by matching the Query of the current token with the Keys of all tokens. The stronger the match, the higher the raw score.

This is why Query and Key are separate. A token asks a question using Query. Other tokens advertise what they contain using Key. The match decides where attention should go.

ComparisonMeaning
Q(it) · K(bottle)high match
Q(it) · K(because)low match
Q(teaches) · K(AI)may be high for taught object
Section 26

26. Dot product intuition

Dot product is a simple vector similarity operation. If two vectors point in a similar direction, the dot product is high. If they are less related, the dot product is low.

Attention uses dot product to calculate how much a Query matches a Key. In simple teaching examples, this is enough to understand the mechanism.

[a, b] · [c, d] = a×c + b×d

Example:
[1,2] · [2,2] = 1×2 + 2×2 = 6
Section 27

27. Scaling attention scores by square root d

In real Transformer attention, scores are divided by the square root of the key dimension. The formula is often written as QKᵀ / √d.

Why scale? When vectors are large, dot products can become very large. Large scores can make softmax too sharp, where one token gets almost all the attention and others get nearly zero. Scaling keeps the values more stable and improves learning.

Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √d) V

Simple meaning:
match queries with keys, stabilize scores, convert to weights, mix values.
Section 28

28. Self-Attention

Self-attention means tokens inside the same sequence attend to each other. The sequence looks at itself. This is different from a translation decoder attending to an external source sentence.

In The cat sat on the mat, the word sat may attend to cat because the cat is doing the sitting, and to mat because that is related to where the sitting happens.

Self-attention = tokens in the same sentence looking at each other.
Section 29

29. Why it is called self-attention

It is called self-attention because the input sequence itself provides Queries, Keys, and Values. The model is not looking at a separate memory or another sentence. It is using the same sequence to build relationships among its own tokens.

Each token creates Q, K and V from its embedding. Then every token compares its Query with all Keys and mixes Values according to the attention weights.

Section 30

30. Every token talks to every token

A powerful feature of self-attention is that every token can communicate with every other token. This makes long-distance relationships easier than in pure RNN-style processing.

For a sentence with 5 tokens, the model can build a 5×5 attention matrix. For each row, one token decides how much to attend to all tokens. This creates a full relationship map.

Token 1 → all tokens
Token 2 → all tokens
Token 3 → all tokens
...

Section 31

31. ATTENTION MATRIX — Complete study

The Attention Matrix is the table that shows how every token looks at every other token. It is one of the most useful visual ways to understand Attention.

Rows represent the current token. Columns represent the tokens being attended to. So one row answers one question: “For this current token, how much attention goes to each token in the sentence?”

For the sentence Deepak teaches AI, a simplified Attention Matrix may look like this:

Current token ↓ / Looking at →DeepakteachesAI
Deepak0.240.090.67
teaches0.090.240.67
AI0.110.110.78

Each row is a softmax distribution, so each row approximately adds up to 1.00. This does not mean the model has selected only one token. It means the current token distributes its attention across tokens with different strengths.

Important: The Attention Matrix is not the final output of the layer. It is the weight table. The model still multiplies this matrix with the Value vectors to create new context-aware output vectors.
Attention Matrix = who looks at whom, and how strongly
Output vectors    = weighted mixture of Value vectors
Section 32

32. Reading the Attention Matrix

To read an Attention Matrix, never read the whole grid randomly. Read it row by row. One row belongs to one current token.

Example row:

teaches → [0.09, 0.24, 0.67]

This means:

Current tokenLooking atAttention weightMeaning
teachesDeepak0.09Weak focus
teachesteaches0.24Medium self focus
teachesAI0.67Strong focus

In this simplified fake example, teaches is focusing most strongly on AI. In a real trained model, attention patterns are learned from data and may be spread across many heads and many layers.

The matrix helps us visualize token relationships, but we should not overclaim that it is the complete explanation of the model’s reasoning. It is one important signal inside a larger architecture.

Rows    = current token asking for context
Columns = tokens that may provide context
Cells   = attention weights
Section 33

33. Manual Q/K/V Attention calculation

Now we calculate Attention manually for one token. This is the same teaching calculation we did in the lesson.

Sentence:

Deepak teaches AI

We use tiny 2-number vectors so the math stays easy:

TokenVector
Deepak[2, 1]
teaches[1, 2]
AI[2, 2]

We calculate Attention for the current token teaches. For learning simplicity, we use the same vectors as Query, Key, and Value. Real Transformers learn separate Q, K, and V projections.

Q(teaches) = [1, 2]

Now compare this Query with every Key using dot product:

teaches → Deepak  = [1,2] · [2,1] = 1×2 + 2×1 = 4
teaches → teaches = [1,2] · [1,2] = 1×1 + 2×2 = 5
teaches → AI      = [1,2] · [2,2] = 1×2 + 2×2 = 6

Raw scores are:

[4, 5, 6]

Apply softmax:

softmax([4,5,6]) ≈ [0.09, 0.24, 0.67]

Now mix the Value vectors:

0.09 × [2,1] + 0.24 × [1,2] + 0.67 × [2,2]
= [0.18,0.09] + [0.24,0.48] + [1.34,1.34]
= [1.76, 1.91]
Final meaning: Before Attention, teaches was [1,2]. After Attention, teaches becomes [1.76,1.91], which is a new context-aware vector influenced by Deepak, teaches, and AI.
Section 34

The previous section calculated Attention for only one current token: teaches. But self-attention calculates this for every token. That produces a full raw score matrix first, then a full Attention Matrix after softmax.

Using the same vectors:

Then the matrix is multiplied by Value vectors:

Section 35

35. Attention Matrix versus output vectors

The Attention Matrix and the final output vectors are different. This is a very important distinction.

ItemWhat it representsHow to think about it
Attention MatrixWeights between tokensWho looks at whom
Output vectorsNew token representationsWhat each token becomes after collecting context

The matrix is like a map of focus. The output vectors are the actual updated meanings used by the next layer. The model does not stop at the matrix; it continues processing the output vectors through residual connection, layer normalization, feed-forward network, and more Transformer blocks.

Core Deep Study — Masked Attention

This part explains Masked Attention fully: why GPT must not see future tokens, how the lower-triangular mask works, and why blocked scores become -∞ before softmax.

Section 36

36. MASKED ATTENTION — Complete study

Normal self-attention allows every token to look at every other token. This is useful for many understanding tasks. But GPT-style text generation has a strict rule: the model must predict the next token using only previous context.

Suppose the training sentence is:

I love learning AI

When the model is learning to predict learning, it should only see I love. It should not already see learning as the answer, and it should not see the future token AI. That would be cheating.

Masked Attention means self-attention with future positions blocked. It is also called causal attention because the model can only use the causal past.
Masked Attention = Self-Attention + no future cheating
Section 37

37. Why future tokens are hidden

GPT generates left to right. During generation, the future token does not exist yet. So training must match generation. If the model is allowed to see future tokens during training, it may learn an unrealistic shortcut.

Training examples from the sentence I love learning AI can be understood like this:

Context available to modelTarget token
Ilove
I lovelearning
I love learningAI

Masked Attention enforces this rule inside the Attention Matrix. Each row can attend only to the current token and earlier tokens, not later ones.

Section 38

38. Masked Attention table

For the sentence I love AI, normal attention would allow every token to look at every token. Masked attention blocks future cells.

Current tokenCan look atCannot look at
IIlove, AI
loveI, loveAI
AII, love, AI—

In matrix form, this becomes a lower-triangular pattern:

Current ↓ / Looking →IloveAI
Iallowedblockedblocked
loveallowedallowedblocked
AIallowedallowedallowed

The blocked upper-right area represents future tokens. Those positions must receive zero attention after softmax.

Section 39

39. Masked Attention math with -∞

Masking usually happens before softmax. First the model calculates raw attention scores. Then scores for illegal future positions are replaced with a very large negative value, conceptually -∞.

Example raw scores:

Current ↓ / Looking →IloveAI
I574
love268
AI349

After applying the causal mask:

Current ↓ / Looking →IloveAI
I5-∞-∞
love26-∞
AI349

Now softmax is applied row-wise. Because softmax(-∞) = 0, future tokens receive zero weight.

Blocked future score = -∞
softmax(-∞) = 0
Therefore future attention weight = 0
Final meaning: Masked Attention lets GPT train on full sequences while still preventing each position from seeing future answers.
Section 40

40. GPT-style causal attention

GPT-style models are decoder-only Transformers. They use masked self-attention because they are trained for next-token prediction and generate text from left to right.

When GPT sees The capital of India is, each head can attend only to tokens already present: The, capital, of, India, is. It cannot look at the future token Delhi before predicting it.

GPT = decoder-only Transformer
Core attention style = masked multi-head self-attention
Section 41

41. Encoder versus decoder attention

Different Transformer families use different attention rules. Encoder-only models can usually look left and right because their job is understanding. Decoder-only models use masks because their job is generation. Encoder-decoder models use both ideas.

Model typeAttention styleUse case
Encoder-onlyCan look left and rightUnderstanding/classification
Decoder-onlyMasked left-to-right attentionText generation
Encoder-decoderEncoder sees full input, decoder is maskedTranslation and sequence-to-sequence

For our LLM learning path, GPT-style masked decoder attention is especially important.

Section 42

42. Multi-head attention

One attention head is one learned lens. But language has many relationships at the same time: subject-action, action-object, pronoun reference, location, time, grammar, and meaning.

Multi-head attention runs many attention calculations in parallel. Each head has its own learned Q, K, and V transformations, so different heads can focus on different relationship patterns.

Head 1 = Attention(Q1,K1,V1)
Head 2 = Attention(Q2,K2,V2)
Head 3 = Attention(Q3,K3,V3)
...
MultiHead = concat(heads) × Wo
Section 43

43. Why one attention head is not enough

A single head may focus strongly on one type of relation, but a sentence may need many relations at once. Example: The teacher gave the student a book because she wanted him to learn.

RelationshipExample
Pronoun referenceshe → teacher
Another pronoun referencehim → student
Action-objectgave → book
Learning targetlearn → student

Multiple heads allow the model to capture several useful patterns in parallel instead of forcing one head to handle everything.

Section 44

44. Different heads learn different relationships

Important point: heads are learned, not manually assigned. A developer does not say Head 1 must learn pronouns and Head 2 must learn locations.

During training, if a head finds a pattern that reduces loss, that behavior becomes useful and is strengthened. Some heads may become specialized, some may be broad, and some may be partly redundant.

Correct mental model:
Different heads can learn different relationships.

Incorrect mental model:
Every head is manually programmed with a fixed job.
Section 45

45. Attention versus RNN memory

RNN memory moves information step by step. Attention allows direct connections. This is a major reason Attention became so powerful for language tasks.

RNN-style memoryAttention
Information travels through hidden statesToken can directly focus on useful tokens
Long paths can weaken informationShort direct paths help long-distance dependency
Harder to parallelizeMore parallelizable on GPUs

This does not mean RNNs are useless. It means Attention solved several important limitations for large-scale language modeling.

Section 46

46. Attention versus embeddings

Embeddings and Attention are different parts of the model. Embeddings convert tokens into starting vectors. Attention updates those vectors using context.

Embedding = base meaning
Attention = context-aware mixing

So Attention does not replace embeddings. It uses embeddings as input and produces richer contextual vectors as output.

Section 47

47. Attention and Transformers

The Transformer architecture is built around self-attention. The original Transformer used attention heavily for machine translation. Modern LLMs use stacks of Transformer blocks for next-token prediction.

Attention is the relationship engine inside the Transformer. It tells each token which other tokens are useful. But Attention is not alone; it works with position information, residual connections, normalization, feed-forward networks, and many stacked layers.

Section 48

48. Attention in next-token prediction

In next-token prediction, the model reads the current context and predicts the next token. Attention helps the model gather the most useful context before making that prediction.

Example: The capital of India is. The model should attend strongly to capital and India, then predict a likely next token such as Delhi.

Another example: Deepak is learning Attention after Sequence Learning, so the next topic is. The model may attend to Attention, Sequence Learning, and next topic, then predict Transformer.

Section 49

49. Common misconceptions about attention

Misconception 1: Attention means human-like consciousness. Not true. Attention is a mathematical mechanism for weighting and mixing vectors.

Misconception 2: Attention weights always explain the model’s full reasoning. Not always. They are useful signals, but the full model includes many layers and transformations.

Misconception 3: Attention replaces embeddings. No. Attention needs embeddings first.

Misconception 4: One attention head is enough. Language has many relationships, so multi-head attention gives more flexibility.

Core Deep Study — Attention inside the Transformer Block

This part places Attention inside the real Transformer block: embeddings, position, self-attention, residual connection, layer normalization, feed-forward network, and repeated layers.

Section 50

50. ATTENTION INSIDE THE TRANSFORMER BLOCK — Complete study

Attention is powerful, but it is not the whole Transformer. In a real Transformer block, Attention works together with other components that preserve information, stabilize learning, and transform token representations more deeply.

A simplified Transformer block flow is:

Input token vectors
        ↓
Self-Attention
        ↓
Add original input back  (Residual)
        ↓
Layer Normalization
        ↓
Feed-Forward Network
        ↓
Add original input back  (Residual)
        ↓
Layer Normalization
        ↓
Output token vectors

Attention handles communication between tokens. Feed-forward handles deeper transformation inside each token. Residual connections help preserve information. Layer normalization keeps numerical values stable.

Simple sentence: Embedding gives base meaning, position gives order, Attention gives context, feed-forward refines meaning, and stacked blocks improve it layer by layer.
Section 51

51. Embedding + position before Attention

Before Attention starts, text has already become tokens and token embeddings. The embedding gives each token a starting vector. But Attention by itself does not naturally know order.

Consider these sentences:

Dog bites man
Man bites dog

The words are the same, but the meaning is different because the order is different. So Transformers add position information to token embeddings. The input to the Transformer is not just token meaning; it is token meaning plus position/order information.

Input vector = token embedding + position information
Section 52

52. Residual connection after Attention

After self-attention produces its output, the Transformer does not simply throw away the original input. It adds the original input back. This is called a residual connection or skip connection.

after_attention = original_input + attention_output

This is useful because Attention adds contextual information, but the model should not lose the original token identity. For example, if the token is it, Attention may bring in context from laptop, but the model still needs to preserve that this position originally contained it.

Residual connections also help deep models train because information and gradients can flow through many layers more easily.

Section 53

53. Layer normalization

After adding vectors together, their numeric scale can become unstable. Values may become too large, too small, or uneven across dimensions. Layer normalization helps keep each token vector in a stable range.

You can think of it as a stabilizer. It does not add new language knowledge by itself, but it makes training smoother and helps very deep Transformer stacks work reliably.

ComponentSimple role
Residual connectionPreserve original information

Section 54

54. Feed-forward network after Attention

Attention lets tokens communicate with each other. After this communication, each token goes through a feed-forward network. This feed-forward network is applied separately to each token position.

Attention answers: Which tokens should share information? Feed-forward answers: How should the collected information be transformed?

Example: in The bank approved the loan, Attention may connect bank with approved and loan. Then the feed-forward network can refine the internal representation toward the financial meaning of bank.

Attention      = communication across tokens
Feed-forward   = transformation inside each token
Section 55

55. Many Transformer blocks refine meaning

One Transformer block gives one round of token communication and transformation. Large models stack many blocks so the representations can be refined again and again.

A useful mental picture:

Layer stagePossible kind of refinement
Early layersLocal grammar, nearby words, simple patterns
Middle layersPhrase relationships, pronoun links, sentence structure
Later layersTask-relevant meaning and next-token prediction features

This is a simplified explanation, not a fixed rule for every model. But it helps show why Attention inside one block is only part of the story. Repeated blocks gradually build richer token representations.

Token → embedding → attention context → feed-forward refinement → many layers → prediction-ready vector
Section 56

56. Attention is learned

The model is not manually told which token should attend to which token. It learns attention patterns through training. If attending to useful tokens reduces prediction error, those patterns become stronger.

For example, the model is not explicitly programmed that it should attend to ball in “it was rolling.” It learns from many examples where such patterns help prediction.

Section 57

57. Attention as memory access

Another useful mental model is memory access. Tokens provide memory items. The current token creates a Query, compares it to Keys, and retrieves Values from useful tokens.

Search → Match → Retrieve → Mix

This is why Attention feels similar to lookup or retrieval, even though it is happening inside vector space.

Section 58

58. Full simple analogy

Imagine a student asking, “What does it refer to?” That question is the Query. Around the classroom, cards say dog, ball, road, and teacher. These cards are Keys. The information behind each card is the Value.

The student compares the question with each card, gives the best matching card the highest focus, and takes information from that card. That is Attention in simple human terms.

Section 59

59. Complete example with it and road

Sentence: The road was blocked because it was flooded. The current token it asks a Query like: what object/place is being described?

The word road has a Key that matches object/place. The words blocked and flooded provide condition clues. So it attends strongly to road.

it → road
Meaning: the road was flooded

Section 61

61. Website demo idea 2 Tiny Attention Calculator

The Tiny Attention Calculator should show the real math in small numbers. It should use Deepak teaches AI with vectors [2,1], [1,2], and [2,2].

The calculator should show dot products, softmax weights, and the final weighted sum. This connects theory to actual Attention calculation.

Section 62

62. Website demo idea 3 Attention Matrix

The Attention Matrix demo should show all tokens attending to all tokens. The user should click a row and see explanation like: “teaches attends 9% to Deepak, 24% to itself, and 67% to AI.”

This helps users understand that Attention is not only one word looking at one word; it is a full matrix of token-to-token focus.

Section 63

63. Website demo idea 4 Masked Attention Playground

The Masked Attention Playground should compare normal attention and masked attention. In normal attention, every token can look everywhere. In masked attention, future cells are blocked.

This is especially important for explaining GPT-style next-token prediction and the “no future cheating” rule.

Section 64

64. Website demo idea 5 Multi-Head Lens Preview

The Multi-Head Lens Preview should show different heads as different lenses. For the sentence The teacher gave the student a book because she wanted him to learn, one head may highlight she → teacher, another may highlight him → student, and another may highlight gave → book.

This should be presented carefully: heads are learned, not manually assigned. The demo is an educational preview, not a claim that every real model head works exactly this way.

Section 65

65. Mini exercise

Sentence: The glass fell from the table because it was unstable. What does it refer to? Most likely: table, because the table was unstable.

Now sentence: The glass fell from the table because it was slippery. What does it refer to? Most likely: glass, because the glass was slippery.

This kind of relationship is exactly where Attention helps the model build better context-aware meaning.

Section 67

67. Final checklist

You understand Attention if you can explain these terms without fear: attention score, attention weight, softmax, weighted sum, Query, Key, Value, dot product, scaling by √d, self-attention, attention matrix, masked attention, multi-head attention, and Transformer block.

The final simple sentence is: Attention helps each token build a better meaning by looking at the right surrounding tokens.
